# FinVerify Code of Conduct

## Our Pledge

FinVerify exists to make financial AI more trustworthy — through deterministic verification, evidence-backed reasoning, and reproducible evaluation rather than blind trust in model output. We believe the community building that infrastructure should hold itself to the same standard of honesty and rigor: scrutinizing claims (including our own), correcting mistakes openly, and treating disagreement as part of the process rather than a threat to it.

In that spirit, we as members, contributors, and maintainers pledge to make participation in FinVerify a harassment-free experience for everyone, regardless of age, body size, visible or invisible disability, ethnicity, sex characteristics, gender identity and expression, level of experience, education, socio-economic status, nationality, personal appearance, race, religion, or sexual identity and orientation.

We welcome contributors from all backgrounds and all experience levels — from a first-time open-source contributor fixing a typo in the SDK README, to a research scientist challenging our FinQA benchmark methodology. Both are doing the work this project needs, and both deserve the same respect.

We pledge to act and interact in ways that contribute to an open, welcoming, diverse, inclusive, and healthy community.

## Expected Behavior

Examples of behavior that contributes to a positive environment for our community include:

- Demonstrating empathy and kindness toward other people, including people who are still learning the codebase or the domain (financial NLP, LLM evaluation, or open-source workflows in general).
- Being respectful of differing opinions, viewpoints, and experiences — including technical disagreements about DVL correction rules, benchmark design, or architectural decisions.
- Giving and gracefully accepting constructive feedback. In a project centered on verification, critique of a PR, a benchmark result, or a design choice is a contribution, not an attack — and should be delivered and received as such.
- Accepting responsibility and apologizing to those affected by our mistakes, and learning from the experience. If you shipped a regression or a miscalibrated benchmark number, say so plainly — the project's credibility depends on that same honesty we ask of the systems we build.
- Focusing on what is best not just for us as individual contributors, but for the overall community and the integrity of the project.
- Being patient with maintainers and contributors working across time zones and in a small-team open-source project — response times will vary.

## Unacceptable Behavior

Examples of unacceptable behavior include:

- The use of sexualized language or imagery, and sexual attention or advances of any kind.
- Trolling, insulting or derogatory comments, and personal or political attacks.
- Public or private harassment.
- Publishing others' private information, such as a physical or email address, without their explicit permission.
- Misrepresenting benchmark results, evaluation methodology, or the capabilities of the DVL/FCG systems — in either direction. Overstating what deterministic verification can catch, or understating a real limitation, undermines the project's core purpose and is treated as a conduct issue, not just a technical error.
- Submitting contributions (code, benchmarks, or research claims) known to be plagiarized, fabricated, or misattributed.
- Other conduct which could reasonably be considered inappropriate in a professional setting.

## Scope

This Code of Conduct applies within all community spaces associated with FinVerify, including the GitHub repository (issues, pull requests, discussions, code review comments), any associated chat or forum spaces, and official project communications. It also applies when an individual is officially representing the project in public spaces — for example, presenting FinVerify at a conference, workshop, or on social media.

This Code of Conduct applies equally to maintainers, regular contributors, first-time contributors, and anyone participating in project spaces, without exception.

## Reporting Issues

If you experience or witness unacceptable behavior, or have any other concerns, please report it by emailing the project maintainer directly at **aaditya.thokal24@gmail.com**.

All reports will be handled with discretion. In your report, please include:

- Your contact information (so we can follow up with you).
- Names (real, usernames, or pseudonyms) of any individuals involved, including witnesses.
- A description of the incident, including approximate date, time, and location (e.g., a specific GitHub issue, PR, or discussion thread), and a link where applicable.
- Any additional context you believe is relevant.

You will receive an acknowledgment of your report within a reasonable timeframe. We understand reporting can be uncomfortable, and we are committed to taking every report seriously.

## Enforcement

Project maintainers are responsible for clarifying and enforcing our standards of acceptable behavior and will take appropriate and fair corrective action in response to any behavior they deem inappropriate, threatening, offensive, or harmful.

Maintainers have the right and responsibility to remove, edit, or reject comments, commits, code, issues, and other contributions that are not aligned with this Code of Conduct, and will communicate reasons for moderation decisions when appropriate.

Enforcement actions may include, depending on severity and pattern:

1. **Correction** — A private, written notice from maintainers explaining the nature of the violation and why the behavior was inappropriate, along with a request for a public or private apology where appropriate.
2. **Warning** — A warning with consequences for continued behavior. No interaction with the people involved, including unsolicited interaction with those enforcing the Code of Conduct, for a specified period of time.
3. **Temporary Ban** — A temporary ban from any sort of interaction or public communication with the community for a specified period of time.
4. **Permanent Ban** — A permanent ban from any sort of public interaction within the community.

Maintainers who do not follow or enforce this Code of Conduct in good faith may face temporary or permanent repercussions as determined by other members of the project's leadership.

## Community Standards

FinVerify's technical culture is built around a specific value: **claims should be checked, not assumed** — whether that claim is "this number is correct," "this benchmark is representative," or "this PR doesn't break anything." We ask the same of community interactions:

- Assume good faith, but verify claims about code behavior, benchmark numbers, and research results before repeating them.
- When you disagree with a design decision (DVL scope, FCG tolerances, API contracts), engage with the reasoning already documented in code comments, issues, or the architecture docs before proposing an alternative — this respects the effort already invested and keeps discussion productive.
- Credit prior work. If your contribution builds on someone else's issue, PR, or research finding, say so.

## Positive Collaboration

We actively encourage the kind of collaboration that makes this project better than any one contributor could make it alone:

- Reviewing others' pull requests thoughtfully, even if you're not a maintainer — a careful review from any community member is valuable.
- Helping newer contributors navigate the codebase, especially areas with known complexity (the DVL's three parallel implementations, the FCG's constraint definitions, the ingestion fallback paths) — a five-minute pointer from someone familiar with the code can save a new contributor hours.
- Sharing findings that make the project more honest, even when they're uncomfortable — a discovered benchmark defect, an unreproduced result, or a security gap is a gift to the project, not an embarrassment to be minimized.
- Celebrating contributions publicly — a merged PR, a well-written issue, or a thoughtful review all deserve visible recognition.
